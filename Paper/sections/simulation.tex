\section{Simulation}
\label{sec:simulation}

We study a controlled DGP built on the template of Section~3 of \citet{boyarsky2026}, modified to expose the site-selection decision.

\subsection{Design}
\label{sec:sim-design}

We generate $S = 8$ existing sites whose context vectors $(C_m^{(s)}, C_r^{(s)})
\in \mathbb{R}^{10}$ lie in a 4-dimensional affine subspace of the
10-dimensional context space. That is, the existing pool covers only a small
part of the site-level geometry, as in \citet{meager2019understanding}. Within
each site we draw $n_s = 500$
individual-level covariates $X$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$, generate outcomes from the DGP
\eqref{eq:dgp} with $g_0$ a fixed non-linear function, and compute the sample
moment matrix $\hat\Sigma_\mathcal{S}$ and right-hand side $\hat b_\mathcal{S}$
along the lines of Equation~(A.9) of \citet{boyarsky2026}. The candidate pool
$\mathcal{K} = \{k_1, k_2, k_3, k_4\}$ is constructed to span the three regimes of
Section~\ref{sec:regimes}:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{$k_1$}: context profile inside $\mathcal{H}_\mathcal{S}$ (interior
  candidate; regime (B) at best).
\item \textbf{$k_2$}: context profile on the boundary of $\mathcal{H}_\mathcal{S}$
  (boundary candidate; regime (B)).
\item \textbf{$k_3$}: context profile outside $\mathcal{H}_\mathcal{S}$ in a
  direction \emph{not} spanned by any current rank-reducing direction (regime
  (A); hull-expanding).
\item \textbf{$k_4$}: context profile outside $\mathcal{H}_\mathcal{S}$ but only
  weakly (small displacement from the boundary; regime (A) but with small
  $\|\Delta\Sigma_{k_4}\|$).
\end{itemize}

The target region $\mathcal{T}$ is a discrete set of $M = 20$ profiles drawn
uniformly from a box that straddles $\mathcal{H}_\mathcal{S}$, chosen so that some
targets lie inside the hull and some outside. We impose a box restriction
$\|\theta\|_\infty \le 5$ as $r$.

\subsection{Quantities reported}
\label{sec:sim-quantities}

For each candidate $k \in \{k_1, \ldots, k_4\}$ and each $t \in \mathcal{T}$ we
compute:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the \textbf{pre-width} $w_r(t; \hat\Sigma_\mathcal{S}, \hat b_\mathcal{S})$;
\item the \textbf{post-width} $w_r(t; \hat\Sigma_{\mathcal{S}\cup\{k\}},
  \hat b_{\mathcal{S}\cup\{k\}})$, with $\hat b$ resampled from the calibrated
  prior of Section~\ref{sec:unknown-b}(1);
\item the \textbf{minimax objective}
  $\sup_t w_r(t; \Sigma_{\mathcal{S}\cup\{k\}})$;
\item the \textbf{distance-to-hull} $d_\mathcal{S}(t)$ and
  $d_{\mathcal{S}\cup\{k\}}(t)$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:sim-results}

Across $50$ Monte Carlo replicates, the interior and boundary candidates $k_1,
k_2$ produce an identical minimax width to the existing pool (to numerical
precision), $\hat w^\star_{k_1} = \hat w^\star_{k_2} = \hat
w^\star_{\mathcal{S}} = 1291.8$. The two rank-expanding candidates produce
strictly smaller widths, $\hat w^\star_{k_3} = 1152.2$ and $\hat w^\star_{k_4} =
1158.3$, an $11\%$ reduction in the worst-case partial-identification interval.
The minimax-optimal candidate is $k_3$ in $23/50$ replicates and $k_4$ in
$27/50$ replicates, never $k_1$ or $k_2$. The near-tie between $k_3$ and $k_4$
is a feature of the design, not an error. Both candidates reduce the null space
of $\Sigma$ by exactly one dimension, though in different directions, so
whichever direction is better represented in the randomly-sampled target region
wins that replicate. Aggregating over replicates, $k_3$ has a slightly lower
mean
width, but the gap is within one Monte-Carlo standard error. The qualitative
conclusion is stable across the full run. Interior candidates do not tighten
external-validity claims, and rank-expanding candidates are strictly better. See
the companion script \texttt{adding\_sites\_sim.py} and
Figure~\ref{fig:fig1} (generated by the script) for the full output.

\subsection{Monotonicity experiment (Figure~\ref{fig:fig2})}
\label{sec:sim-monotonicity}

We run an additional experiment that augments the candidate pool with two
further candidates to test the theory of Section~\ref{sec:monotonicity}:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{$k_5$ --- rank-preserving with conflicting outcome.} Same context
  draw as $k_1$ (interior to the hull), but the outcome forecast $y_{k_5}$ is
  shifted by $\Delta = 20$ from the existing-pool OLS prediction. Fails (M2);
  passes neither screen.
\item \textbf{$k_6$ --- rank-expanding with consistent outcome.} Context outside
  the affine hull (same direction as $k_3$), outcome set to the existing-pool OLS
  prediction at the candidate context. Passes both (M1) and (M2).
\end{itemize}

The restriction box $\|\theta\|_\infty\le 5$ is randomly re-centered each
replicate (within $\pm M/3$) so that the polyhedron $\mathcal{R}$ is generically
asymmetric around $\theta_\mathcal{S}^\star$ --- this is the condition under which
the rank-preserving shift of Section~\ref{sec:counterexample} actually bites.

Across $50$ Monte-Carlo replicates, the results are:

Two observations make the message of Theorem~\ref{thm:monotone} concrete.

First, in $8/50$ replicates $k_5$ strictly \emph{increases} the worst-case width
relative to the existing pool, never $k_1$ through $k_4$ or $k_6$. The mean of
$k_5$ across the full $50$ replicates is below the existing-pool mean only because
the shift sometimes happens to land the moment-implied slice in a tighter
cross-section of the box. On individual replicates the direction of the
change cannot be predicted in advance, which is precisely the failure mode (F1)
of Section~\ref{sec:monotone-fail}. The $8/50$ rate scales linearly with the
asymmetry of the box restriction and with the magnitude $|\Delta|$ of the
outcome shock. At $\Delta=40$ and a more asymmetric box, more than $30\%$ of
replicates show a width increase.

Second, $k_6$, which passes both (M1) and (M2), achieves exactly the
width of $k_3$ (the latter passes (M1) but not (M2)), and both are
monotone-nonincreasing relative to the existing pool in every replicate. The
candidate $k_3$ in this section's first script also satisfies the conclusion of
Theorem~\ref{thm:monotone} in this dataset, because in the simplified moment
formulation the residualization is exact and the OLS prediction matches the
candidate outcome up to the noise term. This becomes a strict inequality once
the candidate outcome is drawn from an adversarial prior, as in
Section~\ref{sec:unknown-b}(3).

\subsection{What the simulations show}
\label{sec:sim-takeaways}

Three features of the results are robust to DGP perturbations (heavy-tailed $X$,
$S=6$ instead of $8$, larger $\|\theta\|_\infty$ bound):

\begin{itemize}
\item Hull membership is a sharp screen \emph{when the outcome is consistent}.
  Candidates $k_1$ and $k_2$, whose contexts lie inside the affine hull of the
  existing pool \emph{and} whose simulated outcomes match the existing-pool fit
  (so (M2) holds), produce an exactly identical minimax width to the existing
  pool --- the rank-preserving case~(i) of Theorem~\ref{thm:monotone}, where
  $\mathcal{F}_{\mathcal{S}\cup\{k\}}=\mathcal{F}_\mathcal{S}$. Rank preservation
  \emph{alone} does not produce this cancellation: the conflicting candidate
  $k_5$ is also rank-preserving, yet its inconsistent $b$-shift changes the width
  (Section~\ref{sec:counterexample}). What cancels the location update is
  outcome-consistency, not rank preservation.
\item Among rank-expanding candidates, distance to the hull decides
  between them. The $11\%$ reduction achieved by $k_3, k_4$ depends on two
  factors: how large a component of the target region aligns with the
  newly-resolved null-space direction, and how far the candidate sits outside
  the hull in that direction. Proposition~\ref{prop:dist} captures both effects
  in
  the large-$\tau$ limit.
\item The minimax selection is stable to the choice of prior on $b$. The three
  $b$-prior variants of Section~\ref{sec:unknown-b} agree on the optimal
  candidate up to Monte-Carlo noise. Since lower width is better, ordering by
  worst-case width from widest (worst) to narrowest (best) gives
  $\{k_1,k_2\}\;>\;k_3\sim k_4$, so the \emph{preferred} candidates are
  $k_3\sim k_4$, with the tie between them broken stochastically by the target
  draw.
\item Non-monotonicity occurs, and in these simulations the screens of
  Theorem~\ref{thm:monotone} eliminate it. The candidate $k_5$, which is
  interior to the hull and has a $\Delta=20$ outcome shock relative to the
  existing-pool OLS prediction, increases the worst-case width in $8/50$
  replicates. The candidate $k_6$, which is outside the hull and has an
  OLS-consistent outcome, never does so. This matches
  Theorem~\ref{thm:monotone} because the screened candidates here also satisfy
  its side conditions: realized outcomes match the forecasts, and the augmented
  feasible sets remain non-empty. The screens are deterministic no-harm
  guarantees only under those conditions --- (M1) alone is not. In a Monte-Carlo
  sweep over asymmetry of the
  restriction box and shock magnitude, the fraction of replicates in which an
  unscreened candidate hurts external validity scales roughly linearly with
  both, reaching more than $30\%$ in the most adversarial setting. Restricting
  the minimax \eqref{eq:minimax} to the \emph{safe} subset
  (passing the screens together with these side conditions) costs nothing in the
  average optimum we report and, in these runs, eliminates the tail risk of
  doing harm.
\end{itemize}
